From the rooftop at AIfA

Astrophotography

When the sky in Bonn cooperates, I take the AIfA rooftop's 35 cm optical telescope out for deep-sky imaging — stacking exposures through r, g, i, Hα, SII and OIII filters to pull faint structure out of galaxies and nebulae. Below are a few plates from the log, with notes on what's actually in the frame.

The instrument

Every image on this page was taken with the 35 cm optical telescope on the AIfA rooftop in Bonn. More on the setup and how the student lab courses use it on the AIfA telescopes page →

M101 — the Pinwheel Galaxy

Type Face-on spiralFilters r · g · i

A large, loosely wound spiral, notably asymmetric — likely the result of a past close encounter with one of its satellites. Faint outer arms are always the hardest part to bring out without losing the core.

M51 — the Whirlpool Galaxy

Type Grand-design spiralFilters r · g · i · Hα

A face-on interacting spiral, paired with its smaller companion NGC 5195. The Hα channel picks out the star-forming knots strung along its spiral arms — a nice reminder of what my thesis galaxies looked like before they lost that structure.

M81 — Bode's Galaxy

Type Grand-design spiralFilters r · g · i

A tightly wound, near-perfect spiral, gravitationally tangled up with M82 and NGC 3077 nearby. One of the sharpest cores I've managed to pull out of a Bonn night.

NGC 891 — edge-on spiral

Type Edge-on spiralFilters r · g · i

Seen exactly edge-on, with a dust lane splitting the disc in two — one of the clearest ways to see how dust and gas settle into a galactic plane.

NGC 7331 — spiral galaxy

Type Unbarred spiralFilters r · g · i

Often nicknamed the Milky Way's twin for its similar size and structure, tilted enough to show off both its dust lanes and its bright bulge in the same frame.

M1 — the Crab Nebula

Type Supernova remnantFilters Hα · r · i

The remnant of a supernova recorded by astronomers in 1054 CE. Hα shows the filaments in the expanding shell.
